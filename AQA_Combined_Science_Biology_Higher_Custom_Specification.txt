1.1 CELL STRUCTURE — IDENTIFYING AND COMPARING CELLS ⭐
Frequency: 8/8 Paper 1 papers (every single year)
Command words: describe, give, name, identify, explain, suggest

Sub-topics tested:
- Matching cell structures (nucleus, plasmid, permanent vacuole, cell membrane,
  cell wall, chloroplast, mitochondrion, ribosome) to cell types (prokaryotic,
  eukaryotic, plant, animal)
  * Appeared: 2018, Specimen
- Identifying a plant cell, animal cell, and prokaryotic cell from diagrams
  and giving a reason for each
  * Appeared: Specimen
- Listing DIFFERENCES between bacterial (prokaryotic) cells and eukaryotic
  cells / plant cells / animal cells
  * Appeared: 2019 (2 differences bacterial vs eukaryotic), 2020 (4 differences
    bacterial vs plant), 2024 (3 differences prokaryotic vs eukaryotic)
  * KEY FACTS TO KNOW: no nucleus (plasmid instead), no membrane-bound
    organelles, smaller, cell wall (different composition), ribosomes present
- Similarities between a bacterial cell and an animal cell
  * Appeared: 2022 (cell membrane + cytoplasm)
- Stating where plasmids are found / what plasmids are
- Prokaryotic vs eukaryotic — standard comparison table

EXAM TIP: Always include at least 4 differences between bacterial and plant cells.
The most commonly missed point is that bacterial cells have no membrane-bound
organelles (not just "no nucleus").

- Give one advantage of an electron microscope over a light microscope
  * Appeared: 2020 (1 mark — greater magnification/resolution)

SECTION D: KEY COMMAND WORDS AND HOW TO ANSWER THEM
================================================================================

DESCRIBE
- State facts in clear, logical order. Do not explain causes.
- Example: "Describe the structure of a DNA molecule" → say what it is, not why.

EXPLAIN
- Give reasons. Link cause to effect using "because", "therefore", "this means".
- Example: "Explain why..." → need mechanism, not just observation.

SUGGEST
- Apply your biological knowledge to an unfamiliar situation. There may be more
  than one acceptable answer.

CALCULATE
- Show all working. Include units. Round to the significant figures asked.

DETERMINE
- Work out a value using information given (e.g. from a graph or table).

EVALUATE
- Give advantages AND disadvantages. Come to a conclusion. Use evidence.
- 6-mark evaluations need: 2–3 points for, 2–3 points against, justified conclusion.

COMPARE
- Describe similarities AND differences. Use comparative language ("whereas", "however").
- Must include data if graph/table is given.

PREDICT
- Use patterns in data to state what will happen under different conditions.

GIVE A REASON
- One specific biological reason. Must be causal.

SECTION G: FREQUENTLY EXAMINED FACTS (Must Know These Exactly)
================================================================================

CELL BIOLOGY
- Plant cells have: cell membrane, cell wall, cytoplasm, nucleus, chloroplast,
  permanent vacuole, mitochondria
- Animal cells have: cell membrane, cytoplasm, nucleus, mitochondria, ribosomes
- Bacterial cells have: cell membrane, cell wall, cytoplasm, ribosomes, plasmid,
  DNA (circular, no nucleus)
- Prokaryotic cells: no nucleus, no membrane-bound organelles, smaller (1–10 µm)
- Eukaryotic cells: membrane-bound nucleus, larger (10–100 µm)

ENZYMES
- Enzymes are biological catalysts made of protein
- Active site is specific to one type of substrate (lock and key model)
- Temperature above optimum: enzyme denatured, active site changes shape permanently
- pH change: alters ionic bonds/hydrogen bonds → changes active site shape
- Enzyme activity is highest at optimum pH and temperature

PHOTOSYNTHESIS
- Word equation: carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen
- Symbol equation: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
- Endothermic reaction (requires light energy)
- Factors limiting rate: light intensity, CO₂ concentration, temperature

RESPIRATION
- Aerobic: glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water (releases energy)
- Anaerobic (animals): glucose → lactic acid
- Anaerobic (yeast): glucose → ethanol + carbon dioxide

VACCINATION
- Vaccine contains dead/weakened/antigens of pathogen
- Stimulates white blood cells to produce antibodies
- Memory cells remain; rapid large response on second exposure
- Herd immunity: enough people immune → pathogen cannot spread

BLOOD GLUCOSE CONTROL
- After eating: blood glucose rises → pancreas detects → insulin released →
  cells take up glucose → glycogen stored in liver → blood glucose falls
- If too low: pancreas detects → glucagon released → glycogen converted to
  glucose → liver releases glucose → blood glucose rises

MENSTRUAL CYCLE HORMONES
- FSH: from pituitary; causes follicle to develop; stimulates oestrogen release
- Oestrogen: from ovary; causes uterus lining to build up; inhibits FSH;
  stimulates LH release
- LH: from pituitary; causes ovulation (release of egg)
- Progesterone: from corpus luteum; maintains uterus lining; inhibits FSH and LH

NATURAL SELECTION (key steps for any question)
1. Variation exists in the population (due to mutation/genetic variation)
2. Individuals with advantageous variant survive better (natural selection)
3. They are more likely to reproduce and pass on the allele
4. Over many generations, the frequency of the advantageous allele increases

CLASSIFICATION
- Three-domain system: Archaea, Bacteria, Eukaryota
- Linnean levels: Domain, Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species
- Binomial naming: Genus species (e.g., Homo sapiens)

SECTION I: WHAT EXAMINERS TEST YEAR AFTER YEAR — EXAMINER'S FAVOURITE QUESTIONS
================================================================================

These question formats recur almost identically across multiple years:

1. "Explain how a vaccine works to make someone immune" [4 marks]
   Key points: dead/weakened pathogen → white blood cells → antibodies →
   memory cells → rapid large response on re-exposure

2. "Explain how CHD causes a heart attack" [3 marks]
   Key points: fatty material in coronary arteries → reduces blood flow →
   heart muscle cells do not receive enough oxygen → cells die

3. "Describe the differences between a bacterial cell and a plant cell" [4 marks]
   No nucleus/plasmid; no membrane-bound organelles; cell wall is different;
   no chloroplast; no permanent vacuole; smaller size; circular DNA

4. "Calculate the magnification of the drawing/image" [3–5 marks]
   Formula: image size / real size. Units must match (convert if necessary).

5. "Determine the concentration of solution inside the plant cells" [1 mark]
   Read x-intercept from % change vs concentration graph.

6. "Explain how insulin and glucagon keep blood glucose normal" [6 marks]
   Full mechanism: detection by pancreas → hormone release → target organ
   response → glycogen synthesis or breakdown → blood glucose normalised

7. "Draw a Punnett square and calculate the probability" [4–6 marks]
   Always: state parental genotypes, complete grid, identify affected genotypes,
   state probability as fraction.

8. "Describe how natural selection could lead to evolution of [trait]" [4–6 marks]
   Always: variation, selection pressure, survival, reproduction, inheritance,
   increasing allele frequency over generations.

9. "Describe the effect of [variable] on rate of photosynthesis using data" [2–3 marks]
   Must use specific numbers from the table/graph. State direction and magnitude.

10. "Evaluate [treatment A] vs [treatment B]" [4–6 marks]
    Always: pros of A, cons of A, pros of B, cons of B, justified conclusion.
